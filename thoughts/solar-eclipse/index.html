    Solar Eclipse
    Aug 31, 2017
    7 minute read

        The day before, a small group of us youth leaders packed our bags and drove from Portland to central Oregon to look for a camping spot to view the solar eclipse. After a couple of hours driving around to check out all our possible options, nothing seems quite right. It was all too expensive for a group of us to simply enjoy the solar eclipse. This meant we have to go to our backup plan and drive deep into the forest to find ourselves a primitive site. After much searching, we were lucky to grab one of the last couple of sites deep in the Detroit Lake at South Perry Campground. There we pitched tent and relaxed for the night.

        That Monday morning, I woke up a little early. Sleeping in the car was good and warm, surprisingly. I roamed around the trail for awhile in my car looking for the ideal spot to watch the solar eclipse. We needed a place that was high and clear of obstructions. As I drove around the bends of the road, it was apparent that the road wraps around the side of the mountain and not the top. So, hiking to the top was ideal to catch this.

        I came back into the campsite, got water and prepared breakfast. Everyone else slowly got up from the cold night. Lexy and Leanna was cold sleeping in the sleeping bag without any padding on the bottom. But they did not so much say a word about how cold it was. I think they were trying to be tougher than they actually are, which is understandable, this being ‘man-camp’ in their head. As we got ready for breakfast, I constantly kept my eyes on the watch. Being the first solar eclipse in my life, I did not know what to expect, at the same time I did not do all the possible researches about it. So, I thought the sky will get darker and darker when the Moon starts to eclipse the Sun. I found out that I was wrong later…

        We climbed up the west hills of the South Perry campground to reach a point at the top somehow. After about 30 min, we reached our destination. Everyone had their own thing to do. Some sat down and started staring at the sun, others ran around to take picture; I was one of those ‘others’. I setup a Gopro to capture the timelapse at 1-sec interval. But other than that I had my DSLR with a 200mm lens ready.

        The moment came a lot faster than I thought. I remember sitting there looking up at the sun when it was about 80 percent covered, and it seemed normal; it was just daylight here. As the moon continue its motion over the Sun, the ground got cooler as I was sitting there, and twilight started all around us. It was a weird feeling, I just remember being frantic. As the eclipse was coming to 100 percent, I constantly took pictures on my camera and tried to look at the sun at the same time. After a little while, I realized that my picture… was and will just be a picture of a white circular dot. So I jumped over to switching my lens to capture a wider angle and to record everyone. I was all over the place! As I was doing this, I quickly realized totality was coming to an end. I did not remember staring at the sun for more than a couple of seconds at a time since I was a bit busy with my cameras. This is something I would do different next time; spend more time enjoying rather than fumbling around with pictures and capturing the moment.

        When totality end, you can see rays of light emerging from the edge of th the eclipse. This was when I relaxed a little and noticed that the last two minutes was pretty frantic for me. So, I stopped everything and decided to sit there and enjoy the last stages of the eclipse. This was my peaceful moment! I was able to just lay there on the rock and just observe the Sun and the Moon doing its celestrial dance. With the final stage, the horizon went from dark to twilight. The hues that surrounds the mountains was just indescribable. Again, I tried to capture this moment on my camera rather than simply enjoy. It was not until about 95 percent eclipse did I really sit down and enjoyed the moment.

        The little things I noticed the air. The gradient of temperature of the air was at the moment struck the awe in me. It was amazing to see how the earth can change COMPLETELY if we were to lack the Sun for a little bit.

        • Solar eclipse moment
          • This was a pretty cool moment. Leading up to that moment was totally worthwhile also.
          • We left the house at 8AM that morning and tried to look for a campsite, traffic was good until we got to Madras, the epicenter of the “solartown”
            • People were lined to get campsites, most of these guys were from California. This was apparent on their license plate
          • We drove out first to Smith Rock looking for a primitive campsite. When we got there, the site was full and cars couldn't go in. So we decided to go to Jasper State Park in Prineville, OR. This was quickly changed to going closer to Madras. The reason being that there was countless of campsite around town; these are sites that farms have cleared their fields to accommodate visitors. With these popping up everywhere as we drove, the option was much better that we might try to find a site closer and pay just a little. But ideally, we dont want to pay for a campsite.
            • Smith Rock State Park -> Prinveville -> Billy Chinook State park was the backup plan that we had
            • We skipped Prineville and went to Billy Chinook, but along the way we stopped by multiple “farm's campsites” to get an ideal of the pricing. It seems like everyone in town is averaging about $25 per person per night. This means that our group of 13 would have cost $200+. We we asked for discount, the lowest we can get it is $150. This was alright… but we did not want to settle for $150 at all.
          • We stuck to our plan and let everyone off at a boat dock and took off into the Northwest region of Billy Chinook lake to South Perry Campground.
            • It was initially disappointing since we could not find any campsites; it seems to us, we were a couple of minute late to reserve a campsite.
            • As we were about to drive back out to the group, God gave me a little nudge to go check out the campsite on the other side of South Perry.
              • Lo and behold, sitting around at a small campsite and checking out the signs for camping regulation, we met the camp host, Scott. He was nice enough to say it was okay for us to park multiple cars at the small campsite. The split second were were about to pitch tent and reserve our site, he drove backward and pointed us to a much bigger and larger site! -> God send! =)
            • So that was that, we got one of the biggest campsite at South Perry campground. Drove back and gathered the crew out to the hidden campground.
          • When everyone got there, we proceed to set up a camp and started cooking food
            • I can kinda see the quarrel between Lexy and Leanna and the other girls at this point. They don't really talk to each other as much, and if they did it was a few sentences.
              • Leanna and Lexy proceeded to take on the task of cooking and preparing food, as the others kinda stood around and waited. After awhile everyone was sent to the boat dock to go explore
            • After dinner, we played Catan at night and went out for some night picture
